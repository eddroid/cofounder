Help! I Need A Tech Co-Founder!

BarCamp Miami 2012


Ed Toro

@eddroid
Will you be my CTO?
No.
  • I don't get married to people I've just met.

  • No offense. Just a generic answer. Your mileage may vary.
Let's face it
  • There are lots of tech startup ideas and very few people capable of executing them.

  • There aren't a bunch of good web developers sitting around bored with nothing to do.
What do you really want?
I want a CTO.
Wrong
  • A CTO is a C-level executive who earns an executive's salary.

  • If you post a job listing asking for a CTO, you could be misleading people.
You want a technical co-founder.
But I'm the CEO. Isn't my first technical hire supposed to be the CTO?
Nope
  • General rule-of-thumb: Don't call yourself a CEO until you're paying yourself a CEO's salary. Until then, you're just a founder.

  • You may not get there. Often the founder of a company does not go on to become CEO when the company grows up.

  • Business culture: The owner of a local restaurant is more likely to call himself a "small business owner".
What do you really want need?
I need someone to handle all that complicated "tech stuff" for me.
Negative

You sound like:

  • ... you don't know what a tech co-founder does.

  • ... you couldn't tell the difference between a good developer and a bad one.

  • ... you will get ripped off.
Who are you?
Case #1: You've got an idea and some money.
You need someone to build a website or mobile app for you.
You need a tech freelancer or development shop.
  • Clear terms: Money goes in one end, website comes out the other.

  • You get what you pay for.

  • Easy to find. Search online, check out local tech events, post to job boards or freelancing sites (oDesk, Elance, etc.).
Case #2: You've got an idea and no money. You need someone who works cheaply and/or for equity.
Have you considered cheap offshore help?
  • Buyer beware

  • Find them on freelancing sites (oDesk, Elance) or through recommendations

  • More tips on this later...

Go on ♥ "dates" ♥
  • Appearance: Work on your business pitch.

  • Location: Tech networking events and conferences.

  • Strategy: Mingle with tech people, gauge interest in your idea, adjust your pitch, repeat.

  • Get married: Woo an engineer. Convince him that you've got a winning idea and they should work for equity to get a piece of the action.

A pitch is a pitch
  • Pitch a potential tech co-founder the same way you'd pitch an investor.

  • Your tech co-founder is giving up as much in salary/benefits/freelance gigs as you'd get from a seed/angel investment.
Case #3: You've got an idea, no money, and no one is interested in your equity.
You're screwed.
You're DIY.
If you don't have a tech co-founder, what's the minimum amount of information you need to get by?
Lean Startup
  • Don't waste a lot of time and money building everything you envision.

  • Learn how to measure how well your business is doing.

  • Quickly find out when your idea isn't working, then change it.

Agile
  • Bridge the gap between business requirements and technology needs.

  • Estimate and plan projects methodically and confidently instead of with promises, negotiations, and "gut feelings".

  • Learn how to manage tech teams.
Learn Some Code
  • Code School: http://www.codeschool.com/

  • Codecademy: http://www.codecademy.com/

  • Treehouse: http://teamtreehouse.com/

  • Learn Code The Hard Way: http://learncodethehardway.org/

  • Lots of free classes.
Make A Website (any website)
  • Register a domain: GoDaddy, NameCheap, eNom

  • Use a site builder: Flavors.me, Wix.com, Webs.com, 1and1.com, weebly.com

  • Use a CMS: WordPress, Drupal, Joomla

  • Figure out the basics of registering and hosting your own simple site.
Learn the difference between IAAS, PAAS, SAAS
  • IAAS: Amazon, RackSpace

  • PAAS: Google App Engine, EngineYard, Heroku

  • SAAS: NewRelic, Twilio, Tropo, Airbrake, PubNub, Postmark... and you?
Delegate
  • DIY until you get some money and/or traction.

  • Write down all the specific things you're doing, especially stuff that you don't want to do anymore or that you're not good at.

  • All the tech things on that list is your answer to the question "What does a tech co-founder do?". That's what you really need.

  • Delegate everything as soon as you can.
FAQ Time!
I already have a cheap offshore team, but I'm unsatisfied with the experience.
Unhappy with the process?
  • Hard to communicate? Ideas getting lost in translation? Big gaps of silence that make you feel uncomfortable?

  • Go Agile.

  • Hire an agile project manager. He'll stay on top of the team, give you weekly/daily status reports, and make sure everything is on track.

  • Much cheaper than a tech co-founder. Could be part-time.
Unhappy with the code quality?
  • Look at you being all judgmental about code quality!

  • Pay a good freelancer or shop to review the code. Don't worry if it sucks. Can it get you where you're going (scale & cost)? Is it viable?

  • Be "Lean". Code quality may not matter in the first version. It's a draft/prototype/demo/hack (even if it's released to the public). Does it demonstrate the viability of a business concept?
How do I pick a freelancer or development shop?
  • Don't hire a recruiter. You can spam LinkedIn all by yourself.

  • Social recommendations: Twitter, GitHub, StackOverflow.

  • Tech events and conferences (like this one): MiamiTechEvents.com

  • Once you know what you need and you know agile, freelance websites seem less shark-infested: Elance, oDesk

  • Find a startup-friendly development shop. They understand Lean and Agile. They can teach you to be a better business. They're expensive and they're worth it. Hashrocket, EnvyLabs, New Frontier Nomads
